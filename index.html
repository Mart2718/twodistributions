01

From counts to inference

A binomial calculator assumes you already know the true p. In real research you never do — you hold one sample proportion p̂ and work backward. The normal model is what lets you turn that single sample into a confidence interval or a test about the unknown parameter.

02

One universal ruler

Voters, patients, defective chips — different units, different stories. The normal framework standardizes all of them into the same metric: the z-score. A z of +1.90 means the same thing in every field, which is exactly why it travels across science.

03

The big-data trap

Hold the sample result fixed at p̂ = 0.53 against a claim of 0.50. The gap never changes. Now slide the sample size and watch the z-score climb. This is statistical significance pulling away from practical importance.

Facilitation notes

The move to make explicit: the effect size readout never moves. Only n moves. Ask students what stayed the same and what changed before they look at the p-value.

Tie to media literacy: "statistically significant" in a headline can mean a real-but-trivial effect measured on a huge sample. Significance answers "is it noise?" — not "does it matter?"
